UMC Reportedly Approached for 2D NAND Foundry Work, But Faces Three Major Hurdles: People, Technology, and Equipment

With international memory manufacturers' exit from 2D NAND flash memory now all but confirmed, supply scarcity has driven a sharp surge in low-density 2D NAND prices. Recent market chatter suggests that UMC (聯電) may receive SLC/MLC Flash foundry orders, raising the prospect that the future oligopolistic supply structure of 2D NAND could be broken.

However, according to industry sources speaking to DIGITIMES, while flash memory foundry opportunities have indeed been discussed with UMC over the past several months, the absence of three core elements — manpower, process integration and development capability, and equipment — has emerged as the most significant obstacle.

NAND Flash Prices Rising Faster Than DRAM

According to supply chain sources, NAND Flash prices have climbed sharply since the start of 2026, with the rate of increase already outpacing DRAM. The supply shortfall is particularly severe for MLC NAND and SLC NAND, where prices for legacy-process low-density products have surged nearly 10-fold. Downstream players report that MLC NAND prices were raised by roughly 30% in April.

Regarding the market rumor that Asian customers have approached UMC about flash memory foundry orders, the industry generally views the current strength in MLC/SLC NAND prices as potentially more attractive in profitability terms than mature logic processes.

That said, existing equipment cannot be used directly, and retrofitting lines or purchasing new tools would take an estimated 1–2 years. Given the enormous scale of the current 2D NAND supply shortage, this would do little to ease the supply gap.

Moreover, the bottlenecks at the execution stage are far more complex than outsiders anticipate.

Hurdle ①: People — The 2D NAND Engineers Are Gone

Industry sources privately disclosed that, in an effort to resolve the MLC/SLC NAND supply crunch, internal proposals were made to explore the feasibility of flash memory foundry work at UMC. However, formidable bottlenecks exist across multiple dimensions — talent, technology, and equipment.

First, potential IP licensors and NAND manufacturers are not opposed to licensing partnerships in principle. The problem lies in the requirement that support must come from manufacturers with actual mass-production experience, including dispatching technical teams to help implement the process.

The issue is that engineers who once tuned 2D NAND equipment have largely retired or moved on, and those who remain have been redeployed to 3D NAND mass-production roles. At present, NAND manufacturers are concentrating their resources on 3D NAND R&D to capture growth opportunities from AI server applications. With manpower stretched thin, they simply cannot afford to take on additional 2D NAND technical support.

Hurdle ②: Technology — UMC's Memory Development Capability Gap

Second, while UMC did conduct memory-related R&D years ago and currently has some idle fab space that could accommodate foundry orders, its core technology has drifted significantly from the memory domain. Re-entry would require enormous resource investment, and rebuilding process fine-tuning and yield optimization capabilities would take considerable time.

If UMC can only provide production capacity without the underlying technology, it would be unable to support customers through process development and integration on its own — leaving the realistic profit contribution to UMC quite limited.

Hurdle ③: Equipment — No Path to Securing Legacy Tools

Third, the equipment problem has no ready solution. International manufacturers' legacy tools have already been sold off or dismantled during line upgrades, and even if used equipment could be located, related maintenance parts and personnel would be hard to secure.

Previously, Japanese giant Kioxia also told DIGITIMES that the discontinuation of legacy products such as 2D NAND was not driven by AI-related capacity crowd-out, but rather by the extremely high cost of maintaining legacy equipment and issues related to equipment aging.

That said, if UMC were to take on flash memory foundry orders and rebuild via new equipment purchases, the implementation process and yields would differ from the legacy approach, likely requiring fresh trial-and-error and development. This implies considerable uncertainty around investment recovery. Additionally, the IC design houses involved are unfamiliar with actual foundry process and mass-production realities — and while their appetite for collaboration is strong, on balance, the idea of a foundry arrangement via technology transfer currently lacks any viable near-term solution.

UMC Notifies Customers of Price Hikes Effective H2 2026

UMC's official spokesperson declined to comment on market rumors regarding flash memory foundry work. However, the company has recently issued price-increase notices to customers and partners, confirming pricing hikes for the second half of 2026.

UMC stated that since the first half of 2026, it has observed robust demand resilience across diverse application areas including communications, industrial, consumer electronics, and AI-related segments. Combined with rising costs across key drivers such as raw materials, energy, and logistics, the company will implement wafer price adjustments in the second half of 2026.

According to related supply chain players, UMC is raising prices across both 8-inch and 12-inch product lines. Based on a roughly 3.5-month cycle, orders taped out in April are expected to be subject to the new pricing.

- 8-inch wafer lines: relatively higher increases of around 10–15%
- 12-inch lines (80nm, 55nm, 40nm, etc.): increases of roughly 5–10%

Adjustments will be tiered based on individual customer volumes. This round of price hikes is primarily aimed at recovering prior price cuts on mature processes. Since some IC players have locked in long-term agreements (LTAs), the impact will be limited, and the increases are expected to be gradually passed through to downstream customers over time.
